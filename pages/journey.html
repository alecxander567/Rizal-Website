Novel · 1887
📖
Noli Me Tangere
✦ Published in Berlin, Germany
"There are no tyrants where there are no slaves."
Latin for "Touch Me Not," this groundbreaking novel laid bare the abuses of Spanish colonial rule and the corrupt Catholic clergy in the Philippines. Through the story of Crisostomo Ibarra returning from Europe, Rizal painted a devastating portrait of a society diseased by oppression. The book was immediately banned by Spanish authorities — which only accelerated its spread.
First Edition · 2,000 copies
Novel · 1891
⚔️
El Filibusterismo
✦ Published in Ghent, Belgium
"He who does not love his country cannot love anything."
The darker, more politically explosive sequel to Noli Me Tangere. Ibarra returns as Simoun — a mysterious jeweler stoking revolution. Rizal dedicated this novel to the memory of three Filipino priests executed in 1872. Unlike its predecessor's hope for reform, El Filibusterismo confronts the possibility that only radical action can break the chains of colonialism.
Dedicated to GOMBURZA Martyrs
Poem · 1896
✒️
Mi Último Adiós
✦ Written at Fort Santiago, Manila
"I die just when the dawn breaks, through the gloom of night."
Written on the eve of his execution, this farewell poem was hidden inside an alcohol lamp given to his family. Untitled when found, it is now his most celebrated work — a deeply moving testament of love for the Philippines and acceptance of death for a noble cause.
Found hidden in an oil lamp
Poem · 1879
🌿
A La Juventud Filipina
✦ Written at Ateneo, Manila
"Rise, O youth of the Philippine clime!"
Written at just 18 years old, this award-winning poem called upon Filipino youth to embrace education and lead the nation toward greatness. It won first prize at the Liceo Artistico-Literario de Manila — the first time a native Filipino was given such an honor.
Prize-winning · Age 18
Poem · 1882
🌊
Junto Al Pasig
✦ Intramuros, Manila
"Beside the Pasig flows, reflecting the pure skies…"
A theatrical zarzuela poem set along the banks of Manila's Pasig River, weaving imagery of Filipino landscape with deeper themes of identity, colonial alienation, and the longing for liberty. It was performed publicly and secretly carried nationalistic undertones.
Theatrical zarzuela form
Political Essays · 1889–1895
🏛️
La Solidaridad Articles
✦ Published in Madrid, Spain
A prolific contributor to the La Solidaridad newspaper, Rizal wrote dozens of essays under the pen name "Laong Laan" (Ever Ready). He advocated for Philippine representation in the Spanish Cortes, freedom of the press, equal rights before law, and the secularization of the Philippine church.
Pen name: Laong Laan
Historical Essay · 1890
📜
Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas
✦ Annotated in Paris, France
"A people who don't know their history are doomed to repeat it."
Rizal annotated and republished Antonio de Morga's 1609 historical account of the Philippines — adding extensive nationalist footnotes proving that Philippine civilization was rich, dignified, and thriving long before Spanish colonization. A landmark work in historical reclamation.
Historical counter-narrative
Medical Science · 1887
🔬
Ophthalmological Research
✦ Heidelberg & Madrid, Europe
A licensed physician specializing in ophthalmology, Rizal studied under leading European specialists. He successfully performed cataract surgery on his own mother, Teodora Alonso, restoring her failing eyesight. He treated hundreds of impoverished patients in Dapitan during his exile — charging nothing.
Operated on his own mother
